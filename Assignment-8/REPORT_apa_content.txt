Keywords: Prolog, logic programming, recursion, family tree, inference

Building a Family Tree in Prolog: Rules, Recursion, and Inference

This assignment models kinship with declarative facts and rules. Basic facts
use unary predicates for sex (male/1, female/1) and the core binary relation
parent/2. From these, derived relations are expressed as Horn-clause rules and
evaluated by Prolog’s unification and backtracking (Sterling & Shapiro, 1994).

Implementation Process

Sibling/1 was defined by sharing a parent: two individuals are siblings if some
third person is their parent and the two individuals are not identical. Cousin
relationships were encoded as “parents are siblings,” with guards to avoid
treating a person as their own sibling and to reduce cases where siblings could
otherwise be misclassified as cousins. Grandparents were expressed with a
recursive helper relative/3 that counts generations downward along parent/2;
grandparent/2 is the instance where the count is exactly two. This satisfies the
assignment’s emphasis on recursion while keeping the meaning of “grandparent”
precise (exactly two parent links).

Recursion and Queries

Transitive closure appears in ancestor/2 and descendant/2: a base clause uses a
direct parent fact, and a recursive clause walks one generation at a time. Such
rules mirror the mathematical reading of reachability on a directed graph
(Clocksin & Mellish, 2003). In practice, the main challenges were (a) ensuring
termination—only finite facts are provided, so left recursion is not triggered
on an infinite domain—and (b) understanding that some rules can yield duplicate
answers when multiple facts imply the same conclusion (for example, two parents
both appearing in sibling enumeration). Ordering of answers follows the textual
order of clauses and facts, which is a common learning hurdle when comparing
Prolog output to informal expectations.

Logical Inference

Queries such as child/2 (defined as parent/2 with arguments flipped) illustrate
simple inversion: the same relation answers both “who are Tom’s children?” and
“who is Emma’s parent?” depending on which argument is bound. More interesting
inference arises with open variables in recursive goals, where Prolog
constructs bindings for descendants across multiple generations.

Conclusion

The exercise showed how small fact bases and a few recursive rules capture
common genealogical queries. The largest conceptual difficulty was not syntax

redundant paths appear.
